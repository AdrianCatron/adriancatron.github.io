A Method of Calibration: Reasoning by Error Elimination

This is a method of the mind, a method of calibration. Its purpose is to discard wrong probabilities faster, because you can be certain they are wrong.

It is almost impossible to know that you are certain about a complex truth, but it is possible to know with certainty that something is wrong. That is a constructed true statement.

If you discard all possibilities that are logically or empirically wrong, what remains, however improbable, must be coherent with reality. It is truth, until proven otherwise. You have, at that moment, the best possible certainty until proven wrong.

This is a rational reasoning method. A rational philosopher begins with basic premises that are impossible to prove untrue. From this foundation, we also define truth as coherence with reality.

For example: ‚ÄúThe sun is yellow.‚Äù

This is a common-sense truth, but it is not a universal one. The sun is not yellow. Animals without retinas do not perceive it in that color. A human being outside the atmosphere perceives the sun as white, because light is white. So, a more precise truth is: Only beings with retinas and similar brain structures, standing on Earth, perceive the sun as yellow. This is a coherent observation based on a specific context.

Every human brain, independent of culture or ideology, shares this interpretation. We call this kind of truth a self-evident universal truth. It is a coherence with reality that, once understood, does not need to be actively remembered, like reading, riding a bike, or knowing that 2 + 2 = 4.
